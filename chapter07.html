üìä 7.1 Two-Sample t-Tests (Independent Samples)

Independent Two-Sample t-Test

Used to compare means from two independent groups when population standard deviations are unknown.

$H_0: \mu_1 - \mu_2 = 0$ vs $H_1: \mu_1 - \mu_2 \neq 0$ (two-tailed)

Key assumption: Independent samples from two populations

Assumptions for Two-Sample t-Tests

  • Independence: Observations within and between groups are independent
  • Normality: Both populations approximately normal (or large samples)
  • Random sampling: Data collected through proper sampling methods
  • Homoscedasticity: Equal variances (for pooled t-test)

Pooled Variance t-Test (Equal Variances)

When to use: œÉ‚ÇÅ¬≤ = œÉ‚ÇÇ¬≤ (equal population variances)

$t = \frac{(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2)}{s_p\sqrt{\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}}}$

Pooled standard deviation:

$s_p = \sqrt{\frac{(n_1-1)s_1^2 + (n_2-1)s_2^2}{n_1 + n_2 - 2}}$

Degrees of freedom: $df = n_1 + n_2 - 2$

Welch's t-Test (Unequal Variances)

When to use: œÉ‚ÇÅ¬≤ ‚â† œÉ‚ÇÇ¬≤ (unequal population variances)

$t = \frac{(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2)}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2}}}$

Degrees of freedom (Satterthwaite approximation):

$df = \frac{\left(\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2}\right)^2}{\frac{s_1^4}{n_1^2(n_1-1)} + \frac{s_2^4}{n_2^2(n_2-1)}}$

üîÑ 7.2 Paired Sample Tests

Paired t-Test

Used when comparing two related samples or measurements on the same subjects.

$H_0: \mu_D = 0$ vs $H_1: \mu_D \neq 0$

where $D_i = X_{1i} - X_{2i}$ are the paired differences

Paired t-Test Formula

Test statistic:

$t = \frac{\bar{D} - \mu_{D0}}{s_D/\sqrt{n}}$

Where:

  • $\bar{D} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n D_i$ (mean of differences)
  • $s_D = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (D_i - \bar{D})^2}{n-1}}$ (standard deviation of differences)
  • $\mu_{D0}$ is the hypothesized mean difference (usually 0)

Degrees of freedom: $df = n - 1$ (where n = number of pairs)

When to Use Paired vs Independent Samples

Use Paired t-Test When: Use Independent t-Test When:
Before/after measurements Completely different subjects
Matched pairs (twins, siblings) Random assignment to groups
Same subjects, different treatments Different populations
Left vs right measurements No natural pairing exists
Pre-test/post-test designs Cross-sectional studies

Example 7.2: Blood Pressure Reduction

Scenario: Testing effectiveness of a new medication

Design: Blood pressure measured before and after treatment for 20 patients

Data: Differences = After - Before

Sample: $\bar{D} = -8.3$ mmHg, $s_D = 12.4$ mmHg, $n = 20$

Test: $H_0: \mu_D = 0$ vs $H_1: \mu_D < 0$ (one-tailed)

Why paired: Same patients measured twice eliminates between-subject variation

Advantages of Paired Design

  • Controls confounding: Each subject serves as their own control
  • Reduces variability: Eliminates between-subject differences
  • Increases power: More sensitive to detect true differences
  • Smaller samples: Often requires fewer subjects than independent design
  • Cost effective: Can be more efficient and economical

Paired t-Test Assumptions

  • Paired observations: Natural pairing exists between measurements
  • Independence: Pairs are independent of each other
  • Normality: Differences $D_i$ are approximately normal
  • Random sampling: Pairs represent random sample from population

Note: We test normality of the differences, not the original measurements!

üìà 7.3 Two-Sample Tests for Proportions

Two-Sample z-Test for Proportions

Compare proportions from two independent samples.

$H_0: p_1 - p_2 = 0$ vs $H_1: p_1 - p_2 \neq 0$

Sample proportions: $\hat{p}_1 = \frac{X_1}{n_1}$, $\hat{p}_2 = \frac{X_2}{n_2}$

Pooled Two-Sample z-Test

Test statistic (assuming H‚ÇÄ: p‚ÇÅ = p‚ÇÇ):

$z = \frac{(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) - (p_1 - p_2)_0}{\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})\left(\frac{1}{n_1} + \frac{1}{n_2}\right)}}$

Pooled proportion:

$\hat{p} = \frac{X_1 + X_2}{n_1 + n_2} = \frac{n_1\hat{p}_1 + n_2\hat{p}_2}{n_1 + n_2}$

Under H‚ÇÄ: $z \sim N(0,1)$ for large samples

Unpooled Two-Sample z-Test

When not testing equality (H‚ÇÄ: p‚ÇÅ - p‚ÇÇ = Œ¥‚ÇÄ ‚â† 0):

$z = \frac{(\hat{p}_1 - \hat{p}_2) - \delta_0}{\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}_1(1-\hat{p}_1)}{n_1} + \frac{\hat{p}_2(1-\hat{p}_2)}{n_2}}}$

Use case: Confidence intervals or non-zero null differences

Large Sample Conditions

For pooled test (H‚ÇÄ: p‚ÇÅ = p‚ÇÇ):

  • $n_1\hat{p} \geq 5$ and $n_1(1-\hat{p}) \geq 5$
  • $n_2\hat{p} \geq 5$ and $n_2(1-\hat{p}) \geq 5$

For unpooled test:

  • $n_1\hat{p}_1 \geq 5$ and $n_1(1-\hat{p}_1) \geq 5$
  • $n_2\hat{p}_2 \geq 5$ and $n_2(1-\hat{p}_2) \geq 5$

‚öñÔ∏è 7.4 Comparing Two Variances (F-Tests)

F-Test for Equality of Variances

Compare variances from two normal populations.

$H_0: \sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2$ vs $H_1: \sigma_1^2 \neq \sigma_2^2$

Test statistic: $F = \frac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}$ (larger variance in numerator)

F-Test Statistics and Distribution

General form:

$F = \frac{s_{\text{larger}}^2}{s_{\text{smaller}}^2} \sim F(df_1, df_2)$

Degrees of freedom:

  • $df_1 = n_{\text{larger}} - 1$ (numerator df)
  • $df_2 = n_{\text{smaller}} - 1$ (denominator df)

For two-tailed test: Critical value = $F_{\alpha/2, df_1, df_2}$

F-Test Assumptions (CRITICAL)

  • Normality: Both populations must be approximately normal
  • Independence: Samples are independent of each other
  • Random sampling: Both samples are randomly selected
‚ö†Ô∏è WARNING: F-test is very sensitive to non-normality!

F-test is often used to check the equal variances assumption for pooled t-test:

üìä 7.5 Non-Parametric Two-Sample Tests

Why Non-Parametric Tests?

Use when parametric test assumptions are violated:

  • Non-normal distributions
  • Ordinal or heavily skewed data
  • Small sample sizes
  • Outliers present

Advantage: Distribution-free (fewer assumptions)

Disadvantage: Generally less powerful than parametric tests

Mann-Whitney U Test (Wilcoxon Rank-Sum)

Purpose: Compare distributions of two independent groups

Null hypothesis: The two populations have the same distribution

$H_0: P(X > Y) = 0.5$ vs $H_1: P(X > Y) \neq 0.5$

Procedure:

  1. Combine and rank all observations
  2. Sum ranks for each group: R‚ÇÅ, R‚ÇÇ
  3. Calculate U-statistics: $U_1 = R_1 - \frac{n_1(n_1+1)}{2}$
  4. Use smaller U for test statistic

Kolmogorov-Smirnov Two-Sample Test

Purpose: Test if two samples come from the same distribution

Test statistic:

$D = \max_x |F_1(x) - F_2(x)|$

where F‚ÇÅ(x) and F‚ÇÇ(x) are empirical distribution functions

Advantage: Sensitive to any difference in distribution (location, scale, shape)

Two paired means Paired t-test Wilcoxon signed-rank test

‚ö° 7.6 Power and Sample Size for Two-Sample Tests

Power Analysis Components

Four interconnected quantities in power analysis:

  • Effect size (Œ¥): Magnitude of difference to detect
  • Sample size (n): Number of observations per group
  • Significance level (Œ±): Type I error rate
  • Power (1-Œ≤): Probability of detecting true effect

Principle: Specify any three to solve for the fourth

Effect Size for Two-Sample t-Test

Cohen's d (standardized effect size):

$d = \frac{\mu_1 - \mu_2}{\sigma}$

Cohen's conventions:

  • Small effect: d = 0.2
  • Medium effect: d = 0.5
  • Large effect: d = 0.8

Sample effect size: $\hat{d} = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{s_p}$

Sample Size Formulas (Equal Group Sizes)

Two-sample t-test (equal variances):

$n = \frac{2\sigma^2(z_{\alpha/2} + z_\beta)^2}{(\mu_1 - \mu_2)^2}$

In terms of effect size:

$n = \frac{2(z_{\alpha/2} + z_\beta)^2}{d^2}$

For Œ± = 0.05, power = 0.80: $n ‚âà \frac{16}{d^2}$ per group
